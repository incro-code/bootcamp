What AI is good for in finance — and what it isn't
✓ Where Claude genuinely helps
  • Month-end close support — consolidates data across entities, flags discrepancies, and drafts the financial summary narrative
  • Invoice accuracy checks — cross-references contract databases against issued invoices to verify amounts, dates, and terms
  • Anomaly detection and commentary — scans reporting data for patterns that deviate from budget, prior period, or peer benchmarks
  • Synthesising board packs and CIMs — turns hundreds of pages of data room documents into deal-ready summaries
  • Drafting investment memos and IC presentations — structures raw analyst notes into institutional-quality narratives
  • Covenant and debt document interpretation — answers "what does this clause actually mean?" across credit agreements and SPAs
  • Forecasting assumption drafts — generates first-pass scenarios from historical context for teams to pressure-test
✗ Where AI cannot be trusted unsupervised
  • Accessing live market data, current multiples, or cap table figures without a verified connector
  • Producing audit-ready outputs — every financial conclusion needs human sign-off
  • Regulatory filings, statutory accounts, or tax submissions
  • Precise covenant or waterfall calculations where rounding has legal consequence
  • Making investment decisions autonomously — it accelerates experts, not replaces them
  • Any task involving raw confidential client data or MNPI without proper data governance
  • Fairness opinions, valuation sign-offs, or any output requiring a licensed professional signature
